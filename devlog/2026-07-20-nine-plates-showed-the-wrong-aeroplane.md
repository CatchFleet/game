# Devlog 3: Nine plates showed the wrong aeroplane

*July 20, 2026*

Every type in the Dex carries a technical plate, a line drawing of the
airframe. A catalogue is only worth the accuracy of its drawings, so this
week we pulled all 37 plate files on file and checked each one against every
type pointing at it. Nine types were serving a drawing of a different
aeroplane. The captions were honest throughout. The images were not.

The clearest offenders were stretches. The 787-9 and 787-10 were both showing
a 787-8, a visibly shorter fuselage. The 777F, a freighter, was showing a
777-200ER with a full row of passenger windows. Anyone who knew the type
could see it; anyone who did not was being taught something false, which is
worse.

So the rule is now written down: **wrong airframe, no plate.** Those three
types have gone back to an empty archival card and will stay there until a
correct drawing reaches us. An honest blank beats a confident lie.

The line had to fall somewhere, though, and it is not at perfect fidelity.
Re-engined variants keep their family plate: the A320neo sits on the A320
drawing, the 737 MAX 8 on the classic plate. Same fuselage, different engines
and wingtips. Blanking those would have emptied half the catalogue to correct
a detail most eyes will never catch. Different fuselage is a lie; different
nacelles is a compromise we can live with and state plainly.

Then the additions. Eleven types gained a plate this week:

- **Airliners.** The 747-400 and the 747 Dreamlifter, which now share the
  Boeing 747 family drawing.
- **Military.** The B-2 Spirit, the C-130 Hercules, the C-17 Globemaster III,
  the F-16, the Rafale and the Eurofighter Typhoon.
- **The rest.** The CL-415 water bomber, the Global 7500 and the
  Gulfstream IV.

Sixty-one of the fleet's one hundred and twenty-one types now have a drawing
on file, up from fifty-three.

Getting there meant going further afield. Most of our airliner plates come
from one Wikimedia contributor, Julien Scavini, whose drawings are excellent
and whose production stopped in 2012. That archive is now fully mined: it
contains no MAX, no neo, no 787-9, no regional jets, no bizjets and no
military types. The new plates come from other free-licensed work, including
Kaboldy's SVG 3-views and scans of US government flight manuals, which are
public domain. Every license is verified through the Commons API before a
drawing ships, and attribution is never invented. We also do not draw our own:
a plate we made up would look authoritative and answer to nobody.

Two smaller things shipped alongside. Enlarging a plate near the bottom of a
long Dex page no longer opens it underneath the footer. And the seven-day
counters now say Last 7 days instead of This Week, because a rolling seven
days is what they have always measured; the old label implied a Monday reset
that never happened.

Honesty section, as promised. Those nine wrong plates were ours, and they had
been live for weeks. No player reported them. We found them by sitting down
and looking, which is the uncomfortable part: the errors were visible the
whole time to anyone who checked, and for weeks nobody did. One of the files
was even named `a320neo.png` while containing a render of the older A320,
so our own filename was repeating the mistake back to us every time we read
it. Filenames are not evidence. Neither are captions.

Next: sixty types are still on the placeholder, and we will not be filling
them by inventing drawings. Some are already queued as requests with
illustrators who take them, and the rest wait for the right free-licensed
work to appear. The plate appears the day it can be sourced honestly, and not
before.
